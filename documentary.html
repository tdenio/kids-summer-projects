Yard Documentary Pick something living in your yard. Plan it, shoot it, edit it — and screen it at the showcase.

Filmmaker: Subject:

01 Pick your subject

What tells a story in your yard?

The best documentaries zoom in on something small and make it interesting. You don’t need exotic animals — a single ant trail or a spider web is enough.

Insects

Ants, bees, beetles, caterpillars. Follow one creature for a whole day.

Birds

Watch a feeder, a nest, or the sky. What comes and goes and when?

Garden & Plants

A flower at different times of day. What visits it? How does the light change it?

Water

Puddles drying up, rain on leaves, water beading on a spider web.

Decay

A rotting log, mushrooms, compost — something breaking down and becoming something else.

The Sky

Clouds moving, shadows shifting across the yard over the course of an hour.

02 Plan your story

A 5-minute film needs a shape

Every documentary has a beginning, a middle, and an end. Fill in each beat before you shoot a single frame. It takes 10 minutes now and saves hours of confused editing later.

0:30
Opening

Establish where we are. Wide shot of the yard. Hook the viewer in the first 10 seconds — show something interesting right away, don’t ease in slowly.

1:00
Meet the Subject

Introduce your subject properly. Where does it live? What does it do? Why is it interesting? Medium shots and close-ups work well here.

2:30
The Good Stuff

The heart of the film. Show your subject doing things — behaviors, patterns, surprises. The more interesting the action, the longer you can linger on it.

1:00
Wrap It Up

End with something that feels like an ending. Pull back to a wide shot. What did you learn? Let the last image hold for a few seconds before you cut to the credits.
